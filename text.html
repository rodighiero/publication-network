The microscope to the Network's telescope: drop in one document and read it in layers — its size and shape, readability, keywords and phrases, and where terms fall across the text. Every layer is computed in your browser; nothing is uploaded.

No text handy? — In Praise of Visual Representation by Dario Rodighiero. Loaded — click Analyze.

Overview

The document's size, and how varied its vocabulary is, at a glance.

How it's built

  1. Split the text into word tokens (Unicode letters/digits), sentences (on . ! ?), and paragraphs (blank lines).
  2. Count each; divide words by sentences for the average length.
  3. Lexical diversity = unique words ÷ total words; “used once” counts hapax (terms appearing exactly once).
  4. Reading time assumes ~200 words per minute.

Algorithm

The vocabulary score is the type–token ratio, a standard index of lexical diversity.

Readability

How demanding the prose is to read.

How it's built

  1. Take words ÷ sentences, and syllables ÷ words (syllables estimated from vowel groups).
  2. Reading ease = 206.835 − 1.015 × (words ÷ sentences) − 84.6 × (syllables ÷ words).
  3. Grade level = 0.39 × (words ÷ sentences) + 11.8 × (syllables ÷ words) − 15.59.

The syllable heuristic assumes English.

Algorithm

The Flesch reading-ease formula (1948), with its Flesch–Kincaid grade-level variant.

Keywords

The content words the document leans on most.

How it's built

  1. Lowercase the text and split it into word tokens.
  2. Drop stopwords (English, Italian, French), tokens under three letters, and pure numbers.
  3. Count the rest; show the top fifteen, bars scaled to the most frequent term.

Algorithm

Ranking by raw term frequency, following Luhn (1958).

Key phrases

Recurring multi-word expressions, not just single words.

How it's built

  1. Within each sentence, take every adjacent two- and three-word sequence.
  2. Discard any sequence containing a stopword, a sub-three-letter token, or a number.
  3. Count across the document; keep those occurring at least twice.

Algorithm

Adjacent word sequences are word n-grams (bigrams and trigrams).

Where terms appear

Whether a keyword runs through the whole text or clusters in one part.

How it's built

  1. Divide the document into 48 equal slices by word position.
  2. For each top keyword, fill a slice when the word occurs in it.
  3. Left is the start of the text, right is the end.

Algorithm

A lexical dispersion plot, in the sense of Gries (2008).
